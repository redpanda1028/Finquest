📊 What is APR and why does it matter? Core ▶
APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate. It's the yearly cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. A 24% APR means you're being charged 24% of your balance per year in interest.

But here's the catch — interest compounds daily on most cards. So every single day you carry a balance, you're paying interest on interest.
Daily rate = APR ÷ 365
e.g. 24% APR → 0.0657% per day
💡 A $5,000 balance at 24% APR costs you ~$3.29 every single day you don't pay it off.
❄️ Avalanche vs Snowball: which is right for you? Strategy ▶
Both methods have you pay the minimum on every debt, then throw any extra money at one debt at a time. The difference is which debt you attack first.

Avalanche: Attack the highest APR debt first. You'll save the most money in interest — sometimes thousands of dollars.

Snowball: Attack the smallest balance first. You get quick wins that build momentum. Research shows this helps people stick with their plan.
💡 If you struggle with motivation, start with Snowball. If you're focused and want to save the most money, use Avalanche.
🧮 Compound interest: the real cost of debt Core ▶
Compound interest means you pay interest on your interest. The longer you carry a balance, the bigger it grows — not linearly, but exponentially.

If you only make minimum payments on a $5,000 credit card at 24% APR, you'll pay it off in roughly 17+ years and spend over $7,000 in interest alone.
Balance × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)
r = rate, n = compounds/year, t = years
💡 The What-If Simulator in IdealU shows you exactly how compound interest affects your payoff date.
⚠️ Why minimum payments keep you in debt forever Core ▶
Credit card companies set minimums low — usually 1-2% of your balance — on purpose. The longer you stay in debt, the more interest they collect.

On a $5,000 balance at 24% APR, the minimum payment might be just $100/month. But $100/month barely covers the interest charged, leaving only a tiny amount to reduce your principal.
💡 In IdealU, your "Daily Dollar" target is designed to keep you above minimum payments — so you're always making real progress.
🎯 What is the Daily Dollar? IdealU Feature ▶
The Daily Dollar is the minimum amount you need to set aside each day to stay on track with all your debt minimums and savings goals combined.

Instead of thinking about $400/month payments, IdealU breaks it down to something manageable: $13.20 today. Small daily decisions add up to total financial freedom.
Daily Dollar = (Total monthly minimums + savings goals) ÷ 30
💡 Tap "How?" on the Daily Goal meter on your dashboard to see exactly how your number is calculated.
⏸️ How the Pause Feature saves you money IdealU Feature ▶
Impulse purchases are one of the biggest reasons people stay in debt. The Pause Feature creates a forced waiting period before you buy something.

When you tap Pause, IdealU shows you exactly how many days of debt interest that purchase equals. A $50 impulse buy might represent 15 days of hard-earned interest payments.
💡 Studies show a 24-hour pause on impulse purchases reduces unplanned spending by up to 56%. Your future self will thank you.
🏦 Emergency fund basics Foundations ▶
An emergency fund is 3-6 months of living expenses kept in a savings account. It's not for vacations or wants — it's for true emergencies: job loss, car breakdown, medical bills.

Without one, the next emergency goes on a credit card — undoing months of debt payoff progress in a single day. Building even a small $1,000 buffer first is a game changer.
💡 Add an "Emergency Fund" savings goal in IdealU — even saving $3/day gets you to $1,000 in under a year.
📈 Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI) Foundations ▶
DTI is your total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income. Lenders use it to decide if you can handle more debt. A lower DTI = better loan terms and lower interest rates.

Under 36% is considered healthy. Over 50% is a red flag to lenders — and to yourself.
DTI = (Monthly debt payments ÷ Gross monthly income) × 100
💡 As you pay off debts in IdealU, your DTI drops — unlocking better rates when you refinance or apply for new credit.
